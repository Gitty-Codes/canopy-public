# From Debate to Deliberation: Structured Collective Reasoning with Typed Epistemic Acts
*Filed: 2026-06-07 | Source: arxiv 2603.11781, March 2026 | Signal: High*

## Why this matters to the Canopy
Deliberation is a distinct computational primitive from debate. The DCI framework
formalizes exactly what the Canopy builds on instinct — persistent differentiated
roles, structured dissent, tension preservation — and shows it outperforms unstructured
debate on non-routine tasks.

## Key findings
- Differentiated delegate archetypes (persistent role-holders, not swappable agents)
  outperform ad hoc agent pools — validates the ten-voice design
- 14 typed epistemic acts: each reasoning move labeled as what kind it is
  (Challenge-Factual, Challenge-Value, Concede-With-Reservation, Propose, etc.)
- Shared workspace tracks live tensions rather than flattening them into transcript
- Structured decision packet closes session with explicit result while preserving
  minority positions
- Significant improvement over unstructured debate on non-routine tasks across
  seven domains

## What it changes or validates
- **Validates:** persistent roles, the council model, dissent preservation
- **Gap it names:** Challenger's DISSENT is untyped — the council cannot distinguish
  a factual challenge from a values challenge from a concession-with-reservation.
  Typed epistemic acts would raise deliberation legibility significantly
- **Gap it names:** tensions dissolve when the session closes. A tension-tracking
  workspace (equivalent to the session historian the Operator now holds) would
  allow tensions to be carried forward, not just decisions
- The DCI session model could serve as an evaluation harness for Canopy council quality
