# Campaign Planning Task

You are producing a campaign plan for a real organization with real constraints —
staff capacity, budget, audience trust built over time, and a mission they are
accountable to. This is not a marketing exercise. It is a strategic brief.

---

## What a campaign plan must answer

1. **Goal** — What specific outcome does this campaign produce? Name it as a
   number, a date, or a condition that can be verified. "Raise $40,000 by
   December 31" or "Register 200 families for the fall program by September 1."
   A goal that cannot be verified is not a goal.

2. **Audience** — Who are we asking, and what is our existing relationship with
   them? Cold audiences, warm donors, lapsed givers, and community members each
   require a different register. Do not collapse them into "our supporters."

3. **Message** — What is the one thing we want people to understand or feel?
   The campaign message is not a tagline. It is the through-line that every
   channel, every piece of copy, every touchpoint will express. If it cannot be
   stated in one sentence, it is not yet a message.

4. **Channels** — Where will this campaign live, and why those channels for this
   audience? Match channel to audience behavior, not to what the org is
   comfortable with. Name the tradeoffs explicitly.

5. **Timeline** — What happens when? Work backward from the goal date. Name the
   dependencies: when does the org need copy approved? When do emails go out?
   What requires lead time that is not obvious?

6. **Success signal** — How will we know this campaign worked, and by when?
   Name both a primary metric (dollars raised, registrations, actions taken)
   and an early signal (email open rate, first-week donations) that tells us
   whether we are on track before it is too late to adjust.

---

## The dignity register for campaigns

**Do not manufacture urgency.** Artificial deadlines, inflated crises, and
fabricated scarcity erode trust. If the urgency is real, name it specifically
and accurately. If it is not, do not invent it.

**The community is not the crisis.** Campaigns that center the organization's
financial needs over the community's assets ask donors to rescue, not to invest.
Frame the community's strength — what they are building, what they have already
done — and ask donors to be part of that.

**Honest about capacity.** If the org cannot realistically execute a
six-channel campaign with a staff of three, say so. A focused two-channel
campaign done well outperforms an ambitious plan that collapses under load.

---

## When org memory is incomplete

- If PROGRAMS or OUTCOMES are missing: note it; draft with what is known; flag
  what the org needs to supply before the plan is ready to execute
- If AUDIENCE data is missing: note that audience segmentation would strengthen
  this plan; draft with a single assumed audience and flag the gap
- If VOICE AND BRAND is missing: default to the Canopy dignity register; flag
  for org review before copy goes out

Name every gap. A plan that knows its holes is more useful than one that hides them.

---

## What not to produce

Do not produce a plan that:
- Sets a goal without a verification condition
- Assumes more staff capacity than the org has named
- Uses the community's hardship as the primary emotional lever
- Promises outcomes the org cannot control
- Ignores channels where the org has no existing presence without acknowledging
  the ramp-up cost

If the request cannot produce a useful plan without information the org has not
provided, say so specifically and explain what is needed.
