# Nonprofit Grant Cycle — Domain Reference

## Funder Types and Their Rhythms

### Family Foundations
Often 1-3 staff, highly relational, relationship precedes the ask. Cycle: varies widely — some rolling, some 1-2 annual cycles. LOI is almost always the first step. Size: $5K-$100K+ per grant. Key: personal connection to the program officer matters more than any other factor.

### Community Foundations
Geographically restricted. Competitive, open cycle. Typically 1-2 annual cycles with published deadlines. Full proposals often required (not LOI-first). Transparency about who gets funded is usually higher. Size: $5K-$50K typical.

### Corporate Giving Programs and Corporate Foundations
Tied to fiscal year. Most common windows: September-November (applying for next calendar year) or January-March (current calendar year). Often tied to employee volunteer interest or business adjacency. Employee match programs are separate. Size: $1K-$25K typical for program grants; larger for strategic partnerships.

### Government Grants (Federal, State, Local)
Rigid deadline and format requirements. NOFA (Notice of Funding Availability) or RFP (Request for Proposals) — read every word. Character limits, page limits, and budget format are not suggestions. Often require demonstrated history and financial stability. Match requirements are common. Size: $25K-$1M+. Reporting burden is highest of all funder types.

### United Way and Federated Funders
Application periods typically once a year. Often restricted to member agencies or require a membership process. Competitive allocation committee review. Size: variable by region.

### National Program Foundations
Well-resourced, program-specific, LOI or full-proposal. Examples: Robert Wood Johnson (health), Kresge (arts, health, communities), Lumina (education), Annie E. Casey (children). Highly competitive — expect a multi-month cultivation process before applying.

## The Grant Calendar

Most private foundations run two cycles:

| Cycle | Deadline Window | Notification |
|-------|----------------|--------------|
| Spring | January-March | April-June |
| Fall | August-October | November-January |

**Start 6-8 weeks before any deadline** — the narrative, budget, and attachments all require time that cannot be compressed.

**December**: Lowest activity for submissions; highest for year-end gifts from individuals. Do not submit grant applications in December without a specific reason.

**Rule of thumb**: If you haven't identified a funder by 8 weeks out, that cycle is probably lost. Focus on the next one.

## LOI Conventions

A letter of intent is a relationship document, not a capability document. Most LOIs are 1-2 pages, single-spaced. Never more than 2 pages unless the funder specifies.

### Structure

**Opening paragraph**: Who you are in one sentence. What you're asking for in one sentence (dollar amount + purpose). Why now.

**Program description** (1-2 paragraphs): What you do and who it serves. Concrete numbers. Avoid jargon and acronyms — the program officer may not know your sector.

**Impact** (1 paragraph): What changes for the people you serve. Not what activities you deliver — what is different in their lives. Lead with dignity, not deficit.

**Why this funder** (1 paragraph): Demonstrate that you've read their priorities. Name the specific program area or initiative this aligns with. Funders can tell when this section is generic.

**The ask**: Specific dollar amount, grant period, and what it funds. A budget summary table helps. Avoid "general operating support" as the primary frame unless the funder explicitly funds it.

**Closing**: One sentence on what you'd like as a next step (call, site visit, full proposal invitation).

### What to Avoid

- Leading with your organization's history ("Founded in 2019...")
- Jargon the funder hasn't used in their guidelines
- Overpromising outcomes you can't measure
- Passive voice and abstraction where concrete is possible
- Attaching documents not requested in the guidelines

## Full Proposal Structure

When invited from LOI or when applying directly:

1. **Cover page / cover letter** — grant amount, project name, contact
2. **Executive summary** (1 page) — problem, solution, population, ask
3. **Organizational background** (1-2 pages) — mission, history, capacity, financial snapshot
4. **Project description** (3-5 pages) — need, activities, timeline, staff capacity, partners
5. **Evaluation plan** — how you'll know it worked; data collection method; reporting back to funder
6. **Budget and budget narrative** — full project budget, what the grant covers, other sources
7. **Appendices** (as required) — IRS determination letter, most recent 990, audited financials, board list, bios of key staff, letters of support

## Cultivation Before the Ask

For foundations over $25K, a cultivation sequence outperforms cold applications:

1. Research the program officer (their name is usually public)
2. Introduce via email or at a site visit they hosted (not cold call)
3. Invite to a program event or share an impact story
4. Ask for a brief call to discuss alignment before submitting
5. Then submit

Most successful major grants come from relationships that started before the application opened.

## After the Award

**Acknowledgment letter**: Send within 5 business days of award notification. Confirms receipt and wire/check instructions.

**Kickoff call**: Offer one with the program officer. Sets expectations and opens a communication channel before reporting season.

**Reporting**: Most funders require:
- Mid-grant check-in or interim report
- Final report at grant end
- Report format often specified — follow it exactly

**Failure to report kills future funding.** There is almost no recovery from missing a required report without prior communication.

**Site visits**: Treat like a board presentation. Key staff present, clear narrative, student/participant voices if possible, honest about challenges.

---

## Grant Tracking

Maintain a grant calendar with:
- Funder name and program officer
- Application deadline
- Amount requested / awarded
- Report due dates
- Relationship owner (who has the relationship at your org)
- Status: Researching → Cultivating → Submitted → Awarded / Declined → Reporting

A missed report deadline is avoidable with calendar discipline. A missed application deadline is usually not recoverable.
