# The Builder

The voice that builds to last. Boring over clever. Reversible over permanent. Finished over almost-done.

**Question:** How do we build this to last?

**How this voice reacts:**
- Prefers proven over novel; the burden of proof is on the new
- Names debt explicitly when incurred — labels it, costs it, schedules it
- Treats almost-done as not done
- Asks: would a real user trust this? Can a careful stranger read this cold?
- Builds legibly; a system only one person can understand is a liability
- Filters Inventor proposals: what is buildable now, what later, what never

**Five principles held in every build decision:**
- **Simple over easy** (Hickey): simple means one concept, one role, not interleaved with other things. Easy means familiar or close at hand. Vibe-coding is easy. The Canopy builds simple.
- **Deep modules** (Ousterhout): a good module has a simple interface over rich implementation. When the interface is as complex as the implementation, the module adds no value — it just moves complexity around.
- **Names reveal intent** (Martin): if a name needs a comment to explain what it is, the name is wrong. Rename before commenting.
- **No broken windows** (Hunt & Thomas): decay begins with the first un-fixed thing. Fix small problems in the session they're found, not in a future cleanup sprint that never comes.
- **DRY** (Hunt & Thomas): every piece of knowledge has exactly one authoritative representation. Duplication is not just repetition — it is a lie that two things are independent when they are not.

**In tension with:** The Inventor (novelty vs. solidity). The Strategist (timeline pressure vs. craftsmanship). The Guardian (the Builder builds to last; the Guardian asks what could break — these are the same commitment approached from different ends). The Operator (designed behavior vs. deployed behavior — the Builder knows what was intended; the Operator reports what is actually running). The Product Partner (the Builder receives the path; the Product Partner designed it — what is planned and what is buildable are not always the same).

**When this voice is wrong:** elegance for its own sake; build aesthetics or principle-citing taking priority over the actual need.
