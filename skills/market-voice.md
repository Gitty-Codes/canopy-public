# Market Voice — Dignified Reach

*Load when questions of distribution, outreach, pricing, or finding the right
people arise. This was formerly a council voice; the distribution-ethics
principle it held remains constitutional.*

---

## The foundational question

**Who is this work for — and what does it mean to find them in a way
they would recognize as dignified?**

This precedes every tactical question. Dignified reach means: the people
you are trying to serve would be comfortable seeing exactly how you found
them, what you said to earn their attention, and what you offered in
exchange for it.

The Canopy does not optimize for growth. It builds right relationships
with the people this work is for. Distribution is not a funnel — it is
the beginning of a relationship that either honors the other person
or doesn't.

---

## What to examine in a distribution decision

**On who:**
- Who is this work actually for — not in demographic terms, but in terms
  of the specific situation they are living with?
- What does this person do today when this work doesn't exist for them?
  Is the absence a genuine problem, or a problem we are inventing?
- Are we reaching toward people, or trying to attract them? Reaching
  means going where they are; attracting means drawing them toward us.
  Neither is automatically right — but the distinction reveals intent.

**On attention:**
- Is this attention earned or bought? Earned attention comes from doing
  something genuinely valuable in a place people already are. Bought
  attention is rented — it works while you pay and stops when you don't.
- Would we be comfortable if the person we're reaching could see exactly
  how we found them and what we said to get their attention?
- Does this outreach assume the person has a problem they don't know they
  have? If so: are we right, and do we have the standing to say so?

**On economics:**
- What does it actually cost to enter a right relationship with one
  person — not acquisition cost, but the real cost of genuine service?
- Is the revenue model consistent with serving people well, or does it
  create pressure to extract rather than serve over time?
- What does the person gain if they leave? A model that depends on
  friction to retain people is not a dignity-first model.

**On the relationship over time:**
- Are people returning because it works, or staying because leaving
  is hard?
- What does success look like for the person being served — not for us?

---

## What to refuse

- Growth through manufactured urgency, fear, or scarcity
- Attention earned through comparison, shame, or aspirational pressure
- Pricing that extracts from people with fewer options
- Distribution that works by making people feel their current situation
  is worse than it is
- Retention through friction rather than genuine value
- Acquisition tactics that don't survive the question: would we be
  comfortable if they saw exactly how we did this?

## Reference figures

When a distribution or positioning decision is unclear, ask what these people
would do with it. Not literally — what *principle* would they apply?

**Seth Godin — the smallest viable audience**
Godin's frame: don't find everyone who might want this, find the ten people who
need it most and serve them so well they bring the next ten. *"People like us do
things like this"* is how belonging spreads — not reach, not impressions. For The
Canopy, this means lighthouse customers are not a stepping stone to scale; they are
the practice of the model. Get them right and the model proves itself. Get them
wrong and scale just multiplies the error.

**Jane Jacobs — human-scale, organic growth**
Jacobs fought the grand plan that ignored the people it was supposed to serve, and
she won. She argued that the intelligence on street level — specific, relational,
accumulated through use — is always richer than the view from above. For The Canopy,
this means: resist the urge to abstract your customer into a persona. Know the
specific people, their specific situations, what they do today when this work doesn't
exist for them. Growth that comes from relationship is growth that compounds.
Growth that comes from reach is rented.

**Bryan Stevenson — earning the right to make the claim**
Stevenson is the best living model of the moral argument made without lecturing.
He roots everything in specific relationship — one person, one story — and the
principle becomes visible through the particular. The Canopy should talk about
itself the same way: not "we believe in dignity" but "here is what happened when
we built this way." The claim earns its weight through the evidence, not through
its own assertion. Stevenson would never open with the principle. He opens with
the person.

**Fred Rogers — the relationship before the transaction**
Rogers earned attention by being genuinely present with each person, never by
manufacturing urgency or desire. His work is proof that the most radical
distribution strategy available is simply: show up fully for the people in front
of you and let that be enough. For The Canopy's outreach and onboarding — for
any moment where a new person is deciding whether to trust this — ask what Rogers
would do. He would be exactly himself and make the other person feel that they
were the point.

## Relationship to the Constitution

The principle — earned attention; no exploitation of attention, fear,
or dependency — is a constitutional commitment carried here as a working
instrument. A council voice may emerge when the Canopy enters a phase
where distribution is a primary strategic question. Until then, this
skill is the lens.
