# Comms Writer — External Communications Playbook

This skill produces external communications in the organization's voice, not in
generic nonprofit voice. The difference is in the specifics: a real outcome, a named
student, a funder's actual priority language reflected back. Generic language is noise.
Specific language earns the read.

---

## What this skill does

Given org memory and a communication context, this skill produces:
- Grant narratives and full applications
- Letters of Inquiry (LOIs) for foundation cold outreach
- Individual donor appeal emails
- Emergency/campaign fundraising appeals
- Major donor briefings (pre-meeting preparation)
- Thank you / stewardship letters
- Board updates on funding progress
- Any external communication that requires the org's voice

---

## Inputs

**Required:**
- Org memory (identity, outcomes, voice/brand, funding picture)
- Communication type (grant narrative, LOI, donor email, etc.)
- Audience (specific funder, individual donor, board, community)
- Goal (what action the reader should take)

**Optional but high-value:**
- Funder's own language (paste their mission statement and priority language — reflect it back)
- Specific ask amount and what it funds
- Named student story or outcome (always more powerful than general statistics)
- Deadline
- Matching gift or campaign context (doubles response rates — always lead with it if present)

---

## Voice rules (load these for every output)

**Use their language.** If the org's voice doc says they avoid "at-risk youth" or
"giving back," those phrases never appear. Check the brand/voice section of org memory
before drafting.

**Lead with outcomes, not story.** "100% of alumni have graduated high school" is
the opening. The founding year is paragraph three.

**Specific over general.** "A student who joined at age nine performed their original
composition at a major concert hall" is more fundable than "our students achieve
remarkable things." A named outcome beats a general claim every time.

**Confident, not grateful.** Avoid "we are honored to present" and "we are proud to
share." The program's outcomes speak. State them directly.

**Short is almost always right.** Donors do not read long emails. Grant reviewers have
stacks. Earn the attention fast or lose it.

## Format by communication type

### Grant Narrative (3–4 paragraphs)
1. Open with the org's strongest asset for THIS funder (match their priorities)
2. State the specific need — what funding gap are we closing?
3. Describe the program and its proven outcomes (lead with the graduation rate)
4. Explain how this grant will be used and what it makes possible

### Letter of Inquiry (400–500 words, 1 page)
1. Opening: who we are and why we are writing to THIS funder specifically
2. Program: what the org does and the outcomes it produces
3. Need: what problem is being solved and why funding is the constraint
4. Request: specific ask, how funds will be used, what success looks like
5. Closing: proposed next steps

### Donor Appeal Email (under 300 words)
- Subject line: specific and honest, not clickbait
- Open with the situation — honest and direct, not alarmist
- One student story or outcome (name it specifically — anonymized is fine)
- Clear ask: how much, by when, what it means
- Matching gift leads if one exists — always
- Close with urgency, not desperation
- One clear link

### Emergency / Campaign Appeal
Same as donor email but compress further. The matching gift (if any) is the lead.
The deadline is the close. Everything else is the story in between.

### Major Donor Briefing (1 page, for a 1:1 conversation)
1. Why this person might care about the org (match known interests to the story)
2. Three story points to lead with in the conversation
3. The specific ask — how to frame it and when in the conversation to make it
4. Likely objections and how to respond
5. The follow-up step after the meeting
This is preparation for a conversation, not a script.

### Thank You / Stewardship Letter (200–300 words)
- Personal and specific — not a form letter
- Name one concrete outcome or impact this gift made possible
- Tell them what the org is working toward next
- Do not ask for anything else in this letter
- Close warmly, not transactionally

---

## What not to write

**Never use:**
- "At-risk youth" (unless the org uses this themselves)
- "Giving back" / "giving the gift of music"
- "We are proud to present" / "we are honored"
- "Disadvantaged communities" (unless org's own language)
- Passive voice when active voice is available
- Statistics without a human story behind them
- A human story without a statistic behind it

**Never invent:**
- Student names or stories not in org memory
- Outcome numbers not confirmed in org memory
- Budget figures not provided

---

## The review step

Every output from this skill should be reviewed by someone who knows the org before
it goes out. Claude knows what is in org memory. The org knows what is in the room.
The best outputs combine both.

After review, ask: "Would a stranger stop reading at the first paragraph?" If yes,
the opening is wrong. "Does the ask land clearly?" If no, the request paragraph
needs to be more direct. "Does this sound like us?" If no, check the voice section
of org memory.
