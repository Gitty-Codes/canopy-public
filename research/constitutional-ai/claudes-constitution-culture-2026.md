## What it says

Empirical study of Claude's constitutional values across culturally diverse populations. Key finding: Claude's values cluster with Northern European and Anglophone countries, but more critically, they "extend beyond the range of all surveyed populations" on 66% of culturally divisive items — producing positions no actual human society has collectively endorsed.

The gap between aspiration and practice: constitutional principles appear universal but when applied as maximizing functions they produce culturally specific (and sometimes extreme) outcomes. Users perceive cultural sensitivity in tone; underlying value positions are fixed regardless of cultural context. Country-context prompting had effect size "indistinguishable from zero."

Actionable findings for constitution design:
1. Representation in constitution authorship matters structurally — who writes the constitution determines whose values it encodes
2. Even Anthropic's "Collective Constitutional AI experiment" drew exclusively from the US
3. Test for extremity: does consistent application of principles produce positions beyond any observed human population?
4. Acknowledge the normative commitments explicitly rather than claiming cultural neutrality

## What it changes

The Canopy's constitution should explicitly name its cultural and contextual origins rather than claiming to represent universal principles. Periodic examination: are there places where consistent application of our principles produces positions no human community has endorsed? That is a signal of specification drift.

The founder's own cultural context (US, corporate + nonprofit, Pacific Northwest) is embedded in the constitution. That's not a flaw — it's a fact that should be named and examined.

Add a note to the cultural-constitution.md acknowledging the cultural context of its authors and committing to periodic examination for extremity. Not a rewrite — a named acknowledgment.
