# Nonprofit Formation — Domain Reference

## Two Paths

### Path A: Fiscal Sponsorship

A fiscal sponsor is an existing 501(c)(3) that accepts your project under its legal umbrella. You operate under their tax-exempt status; they handle compliance, and take a fee (typically 5-12% of revenue).

**Use when:**
- Early stage, uncertain longevity, or testing an idea
- Budget is under $100K/year and admin burden of a standalone nonprofit would distract
- Speed matters — can be operational in weeks, not months
- The mission fits an existing sponsor's portfolio

**Tradeoffs:**
- Less autonomy (sponsor can terminate the arrangement)
- Fee reduces program dollars
- Cannot hold your own assets long-term
- Sponsor's reputation is your reputation

Well-known fiscal sponsors: Fractured Atlas (arts/social good), IOBY (community projects), Social Good Fund (broad portfolio).

### Path B: Direct 501(c)(3) Formation

Full legal independence. The organization holds its own assets, enters its own contracts, and is solely accountable for its compliance.

**Use when:**
- Established mission with multi-year intent
- Budget is growing past $150K+/year where sponsor fees become significant
- Need to hold property, employ staff directly, or receive certain types of government funding
- Long-term independence is part of the mission

## Direct Formation — Step by Step

### Step 1: Choose a State of Incorporation

Typically your home state. Delaware and Nevada offer formation advantages for for-profits; for nonprofits, your home state is almost always correct. Filing in Delaware adds a registered agent cost with no meaningful benefit.

### Step 2: Draft Articles of Incorporation

Must include:
- Organization name and principal office address
- Statement of purpose (must be charitable, educational, religious, or scientific per IRS §501(c)(3))
- **IRS-required language**: "No part of the net earnings of the corporation shall inure to the benefit of, or be distributable to, its members, trustees, officers, or other private persons..."
- **Dissolution clause**: On dissolution, remaining assets must go to another 501(c)(3) — never to individuals
- Initial registered agent

Cost: $50-200 depending on state. Many states have an online filing portal.

### Step 3: Draft Bylaws

Bylaws govern internal operations. Required sections:
- Board composition (minimum 3 directors, recommended 5-9)
- Director terms and term limits
- Officer roles (President/Chair, Secretary, Treasurer at minimum)
- Meeting requirements (frequency, quorum)
- Voting procedures
- Amendment process

The IRS does not receive bylaws at formation, but will ask for them in the Form 1023 review. Write them now.

### Step 4: Conflict of Interest Policy

The IRS strongly recommends (and functionally requires) this for 1023 approval. It must cover:
- Definition of conflict (financial interest in a transaction)
- Disclosure procedure
- Recusal from voting
- Annual attestation by board members

Many state-specific templates are available from National Council of Nonprofits.

### Step 5: Obtain an EIN

Employer Identification Number — free, immediate, online at IRS.gov (even without employees). Required before filing Form 1023.

### Step 6: Hold an Organizational Board Meeting

Document in minutes:
- Board members present
- Election of initial officers
- Adoption of bylaws
- Adoption of conflict of interest policy
- Authorization to apply for tax-exempt status

These minutes are an IRS requirement.

### Step 7: File Form 1023-EZ or Form 1023

**Form 1023-EZ** (shorter, $275 fee, typically 2-4 week approval):
- Projected annual gross receipts: under $50,000 for the next 3 years
- Total assets: under $250,000
- Not a church, school, hospital, or supporting organization

**Form 1023** (full application, $600 fee, 3-12 month review):
- Required if 1023-EZ eligibility is not met
- Requires narrative: activities description, compensation disclosure, financial data

File at pay.gov.

### Step 8: State Charitable Solicitation Registration

Most states require registration before soliciting donations. This is separate from federal tax exemption. Requirements vary by state — Bolder Advocacy and National Council of Nonprofits maintain state-by-state guides.

## Timeline

| Milestone | Typical Duration |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Articles filed | 1-5 business days |
| EIN obtained | Immediate (online) |
| 1023-EZ approved | 2-4 weeks |
| 1023 (full) approved | 3-12 months |
| State solicitation registration | 2-8 weeks |

IRS approval is retroactive to the date of incorporation if the application is filed within 27 months.

## Board Requirements

- Minimum 3 directors (IRS does not set a minimum, but most states do, and IRS scrutinizes smaller boards)
- Majority must be **unrelated** (not family members or spouses of other directors)
- Majority must be **uncompensated** (officers can be paid, but majority of board must be unpaid)
- Staff should never dominate the board — ideally zero staff as voting board members

**Red flags the IRS looks for:**
- Founder control (founder holds majority votes or all officer positions)
- Compensation to insiders without documented independent approval
- Private benefit (org activities primarily benefiting the founders)

---

## Governance Gate

Formation is irreversible in the sense that it establishes legal obligations that persist. Before filing:
- Articles of incorporation: review with an attorney or qualified nonprofit consultant
- Bylaws: review with an attorney
- Form 1023: strongly recommend professional review — errors delay approval by months

Filing the articles is an **irreversible** action under the governance gate protocol.

---

## What to Do After Approval

- Open a bank account in the organization's name (requires articles + EIN)
- Establish accounting practices and chart of accounts
- File annual 990 (due 4.5 months after fiscal year end — late filing triggers penalties)
- Maintain board meeting minutes every time the board meets
- Register for any required state/local permits
