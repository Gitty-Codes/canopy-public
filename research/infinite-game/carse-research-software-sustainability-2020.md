# Finite and Infinite Games: An Ethnography of Institutional Logics in Research Software Sustainability
*Filed: 2026-06-07 | Source: Proceedings of ASIST 2020, DOI 10.1002/pra2.281 | Signal: High*

## Why this matters to the Canopy
First peer-reviewed application of Carse's finite/infinite game framework to
institutional technology design. Treats the game structure itself — the rules
institutions impose — as the variable to change, not the optimization within those rules.

## Key findings
- 10-year ethnography of two large research software projects (astronomy,
  macromolecular modeling)
- Funding agency structures encode finite game logic (win conditions, milestones,
  defined endpoints) while projects that survived operated as infinite game
  institutions — playing to continue play rather than to win
- Institutional sustainability increases when governance resembles infinite game
  design: continuation over victory, participation over winning
- Direct policy implication: funders can change game structure, not just optimize
  within it

## What it changes or validates
- **Validates:** the Canopy's direction note — "use game theory to understand the
  game, then break it open" — has academic grounding in institutional design
- **The dignity-first parallel:** a system designed to "continue play" rather than
  "win" mirrors the Canopy's framing that dignity and sustainability are generative,
  not instrumental. This is the same insight from a different domain
- **Note:** no equivalent work applies Carse directly to AI systems design —
  that gap remains. This is the academic entry point; direct engagement with the
  primary text (Carse 1986) remains warranted alongside it
- **Practical application:** the Practice Buddy work is infinite game
  institutional design — serving underserved students is play that continues play,
  not optimization toward a win condition
