## What it says

Constitutional AI, RLHF, inverse reinforcement learning, and cooperative assistance games all fail structurally — not as engineering limitations. The failure mode: they treat values as fixed specifications. Systems optimize the literal rules rather than underlying intentions, and always break at novel situations no specification anticipated. The paper calls this the "specification trap" — the impossibility of capturing complete, context-sensitive moral values in any finite upfront specification.

Proposed alternative: dynamic value learning that adapts to context; ongoing human-AI deliberation instead of one-time specification; accepting irreducible uncertainty in value alignment rather than pretending completeness is achievable.

## What it changes

The Canopy's approach — decisions-log, voice amendments, council deliberation, living constitution — is already what this paper advocates. We are doing Wide Reflective Equilibrium (see companion file) rather than specification. This paper gives us language for why: static specification is a trap; deliberation is the alternative.

The implication for our work: do not move toward hardcoded rules. Move toward better deliberation. The values-as-reflex question must not be answered by "codify the values and load them always" — that is the specification trap. It should be answered by "make the deliberation faster and more contextually sensitive."
